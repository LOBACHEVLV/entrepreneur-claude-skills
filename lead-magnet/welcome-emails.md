## Email 2: The Skill Most People Start With (Day 2)

**Subject:** Start here (not where you'd think)
**Preview text:** The most useful skill in the collection isn't copywriting

Most people who download the playbook go straight to the copywriting or landing page skills. Makes sense — those feel immediately useful.

But the skill that changes how you think is the **offer-creation** skill.

Here's why: most founders start with "what should my website say?" when the real question is "what am I actually selling?"

The offer-creation skill uses the Hormozi value equation to restructure your product into an offer that makes saying no feel irrational. Value stacking. Guarantees. Naming. The whole framework.

Try it. Open Claude Code and say: "Help me create an irresistible offer for [your product]."

The output usually surprises people. It forces you to think about your product from the buyer's perspective — what outcome they want, what risk they perceive, and why your thing is the obvious choice.

Once you have a clear offer, every other skill (copy, landing page, emails, ads) gets 10x easier because you know exactly what you're selling.

## Email 4: The One Skill I Wish I Had Earlier (Day 8)

**Subject:** I lost $40K because I skipped this
**Preview text:** The skill I built after an expensive mistake

Quick story.

Two years ago I spent $40K on a product launch that flopped. The product was fine. The marketing was fine. The problem: I never ran the unit economics. CAC was 3x what I'd assumed because I was targeting the wrong channel.

If I'd spent 30 minutes with a unit economics model before launching, I would have caught it. Instead, I caught it after burning through the budget.

That's why the **unit-economics** skill exists in the collection. You plug in your numbers — spend, revenue, COGS, churn — and it calculates CAC, LTV, payback period, and break-even. Then it runs scenarios: "What if you cut CAC by 20%? What if churn drops 2%?"

It also tells you when your numbers are bad. Which is the whole point — catching problems before they become expensive.

Try it: "Analyze my unit economics. Here's what I know: [your numbers]."

Even rough estimates are better than vibes.
